Easeparenting
and institutional design

How structural constraints and algorithmic convenience shape parental choice—and why this threatens epistemic capacity across a generation.

Original Publication May 15, 2026
Author Md. Miraz Hossain
Status Original Concept
Context TBS Reporting (2026)

A missing term

In January 2026, I was reporting on children's screen time usage across Dhaka. The pattern was unexpected: families with greater financial resources were using significantly more screens than lower-income families—not less.

Wealthy parents were deliberately choosing screens over active engagement. This contradicted everything the narrative had told us about screen time as a poverty problem.

The answer required a term that didn't yet exist.

Easeparenting (Definition)

The deliberate parental choice to substitute digital screens and algorithmic convenience for active engagement and real-world interaction, in order to reduce parental workload and cognitive burden.

This is not negligence. This is not addiction. This is rational choice—constrained choice, made within structural conditions that make ease feel like the only option.

The data tells a different story

The icddr,b conducted a comprehensive survey on children's screen time in 2025. The findings were stark—and they prove that screen use is a choice of the wealthy, not a function of poverty.

If screens were a poverty substitute, lower-income families would use more. They don't. Wealthier families choose screens because screens work. They reduce parental burden. They maintain compliance without negotiation.

Why this happens

Easeparenting is not irrational. Parents are responding to genuine structural constraints that make ease a rational choice.

This distinction matters for policy. If the problem is individual parents, the solution is guilt and judgment. If the problem is structural design, the solution is system redesign.

Work Intensification

Urban employment demands longer hours and greater cognitive load. Parents return home exhausted.

Childcare Collapse

Formal childcare is expensive and limited. Extended family networks have fractured due to urbanization.

Urban Isolation

Apartment living and traffic make outdoor play impossible. Parents feel individually responsible for entertainment.

The epistemic cost

The activities displaced by easeparenting are precisely those that build epistemic capacity—the ability to evaluate evidence, ask critical questions, and resist manipulation.

When children spend 4.6 hours daily on algorithmic media, they lose: unstructured play, conversation with adults, constraint and boredom, embodied learning.

Algorithmic media does the opposite: it rewards immediate gratification, bypasses questioning, eliminates boredom, and is entirely passive.

Algorithmic epistemic vulnerability

A condition in which the child's capacity for critical thought has been systematically eroded, not through parental cruelty, but through the displacement of developmental activities. The problem is not the child's compulsion—it is the parent's deliberate choice to enable it.

International evidence: policy reversals

Several wealthy nations tried going digital-first. They have systematically reversed course after measuring long-term impacts.

  • France (2018): Banned smartphones from elementary schools
  • South Korea (2020): Reversed to paper-based learning
  • Sweden (2023): Abandoned digital-heavy education
  • Italy (2024): Restricted AI-generated imagery in schools

These are not Luddite countries rejecting technology. They are wealthy, digitally sophisticated nations that measured the cost and chose to reverse it.

Bangladesh is accelerating into the trap these countries escaped.

Individual vs. Structural

Individual Blame

"Parents who use screens are lazy, incompetent, or don't care about their children."

Structural Approach

"Parents are rational actors responding to systems that make ease the only option. Change the systems."

This distinction matters for policy. Most interventions target the individual. That is why they fail.

Implications for education

If easeparenting is systematic—if it reflects structural constraints rather than parental pathology—then schools cannot solve it through curriculum alone.

But schools can respond by centering epistemic formation: teaching question-asking, evidence evaluation, tolerance for uncertainty. Not as a module, but as the foundation of all learning.

Schools need to:

  • ✓ Center epistemic formation as foundation of all learning
  • ✓ Create developmental time for unstructured play and embodied learning
  • ✓ Limit screens in school to prevent doubling of algorithmic exposure
  • ✓ Address structural factors through policy: childcare, work options, urban design

Why this term matters

Coining "easeparenting" does three critical things:

  1. Names a phenomenon without blame. It describes what is happening, not who is bad.
  2. Shifts responsibility to systems. The problem is not parents—it is work structure, childcare infrastructure, app design, educational policy.
  3. Makes policy intervention possible. You cannot solve what you cannot name. Once named, you can ask: What structural changes would reduce easeparenting?

Bangladesh's children are not being harmed by bad parents. They are being harmed by systems optimized for extraction—extraction of parental labor, extraction of children's attention, extraction of epistemic capacity.

The question is not how to make parents feel guiltier. The question is how to redesign systems so that active parenting and critical thinking become the path of least resistance—not the luxury that only guilt-ridden, resourced parents can afford.
